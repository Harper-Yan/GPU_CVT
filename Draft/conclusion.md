# 6. Conclusion

We presented a curvature-adaptive site-freezing strategy for GPU-accelerated surface CVT via Lloyd iteration. The method is grounded in a complete causal analysis of convergence failure on curved surfaces: tangent-plane distortion biases centroid computation, causing persistent oscillation and asynchronous neighborhood convergence at high curvature. These effects make convergence detection unreliable when based on displacement alone, with 37% of low-displacement moments at sharp regions exhibiting unstable neighborhoods.

Our dual-gate freeze policy addresses both failure modes. Gate 1 (low displacement) detects when a site has stopped moving. Gate 2 (KNN stability) detects when the local Voronoi topology has stabilized. Both gates must pass for a curvature-dependent number of consecutive iterations before a site is frozen, with stricter requirements where the tangent-plane approximation is least reliable. Once frozen, a site's KNN queries are skipped while centroid and projection updates continue, allowing frozen sites to track ongoing neighbor movement.

On a benchmark of 12 meshes spanning 35K to 544K vertices, the policy achieves 1.2x to 8.3x speedup with less than 0.1% degradation in average element quality. The largest gains occur on large meshes where baseline GPU CVT is most expensive: the 173K-vertex Armadillo drops from 93 seconds to 26 seconds; the 544K-vertex Happy Buddha drops from 14.4 minutes to 1.7 minutes. The 6-tier variant, which distinguishes singularity sites from ridge sites within the sharp curvature tier, further improves performance on meshes with bimodal sharp-tier populations.

The freeze policy is a drop-in modification to any tangent-plane Lloyd iteration loop. It requires no changes to the per-site update rule, no additional mesh queries beyond what the Lloyd loop already computes, and no global synchronization. The curvature tiering is computed once from existing KNN normals; the per-iteration freeze test adds two comparisons (displacement and neighbor identity) and one counter increment per unfrozen site.

Several directions remain open. A formal analysis of CVT energy behavior under partial site freezing would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Sensitivity analysis of tier boundaries and streak lengths would quantify robustness. Extending the policy to skip centroid and projection for sites whose entire neighborhood is frozen could further reduce per-iteration cost. Combining the freeze policy with faster-converging solvers (L-BFGS, Anderson acceleration) could yield compounding gains: fewer iterations needed, and each iteration cheaper. Finally, adapting the curvature-dependent convergence analysis to anisotropic CVT or restricted Voronoi diagram frameworks would broaden the applicability beyond tangent-plane Lloyd iteration.
