# Abstract

Surface CVT via Lloyd iteration is widely used for isotropic remeshing. In practice, most sites reach a state of operational convergence, where both displacement and local neighborhood topology stabilize, well before the assigned iteration number is reached. However, most exsiting methods uniformly recomputes KNN, centroids, and projections for all sites at each iteration, wasting the majority of work on sites that have already settled.

We identify a previously uncharacterized relationship between surface curvature and per-site convergence behavior in Lloyd iteration that has direct implications for CVT workload reduction. On flat regions, the tangent-plane approximation is accurate, centroids computation are unbiased, and sites converge monotonically; a short streak of low displacement reliably indicates convergence, and the corresponding GPU work can be safely eliminated. On curved regions, tangent-plane distortion biases centroid computation, causing sites to oscillate persistently and converge at vastly different rates. Neighboring sites oscillate with independent phases, so a site may appear momentarily stationary while its local Voronoi geometry is still being reshaped. Meaning displacement alone cannot reliably identify which sites are safe to exclude from computation, and the stability of neighborhood landscape must be considered as well. 

This curvature-convergence analysis provides a basis for non-uniform GPU workload allocation in iterative surface optimization, the curvature-convergence relationship is exploited to accelerate GPU CVT. A site is declared converged and frozen, permanently excluded from KNN queries, only when both low displacement and stable KNN topology are sustained over a number of consecutive iterations scaled by local curvature. Stricter criteria at high curvature prevent false convergence; relaxed criteria at low curvature allow early freezing where convergence is reliable. Centroid and projection updates continue for all sites including frozen ones, allowing them to track ongoing neighbor movement. Since KNN search dominates GPU cost, skipping it for frozen sites yields speedup proportional to the frozen fraction.

We evaluate on meshes ranging from 35K to over 1.26M vertices. To our knowledge, no prior CVT remeshing work has reported results beyond roughly 100K vertices; our experiments exceed this scale by an order of magnitude. On large meshes our method achieves 3.5x to 8.3x speedup (e.g., 173K Armadillo: 93s to 26s; 544K Happy Buddha: 14.4 min to 1.7 min). At the 1.26M-vertex scale (Lucy), standard GPU CVT is impractical sicne a single Lloyd iteration takes over 50 seconds, whereas our method completes 250 iterations in under 4 minutes by freezing 88% of sites. Across models, the mesh quality measured by aspect ratio and Hausdorff distance are comparable to none-work-skipping baselines.
